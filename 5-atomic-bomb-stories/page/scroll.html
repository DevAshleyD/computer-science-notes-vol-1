By Minami Funakoshi

Published August 13, 2019

On August 6, 1945, at 8:15 am, the United States dropped the worldâ€™s first atomic bomb on Hiroshima. Buildings crumbled; homes exploded into flames; flesh melted. The heat branded the patterns of the kimono onto the skin of survivors. Some were vapourised, leaving nothing but a shadow etched onto stone.

Three days later, at 11:02 am on August 9, the United States dropped a second atomic bomb on the port city of Nagasaki.

The bombs killed thousands instantly. Others died slowly from burns, radiation sickness and other injuries in the months and years that followed.

In 1952, seven years after the bombing of Hiroshima, the city began compiling a list of those who had been killed by the atomic bomb. Nagasaki city began its own list in 1968, over two decades after the bombings.

Every year, on August 6 and 9, Hiroshima and Nagasaki hold memorial services to rememeber those killed by the bombs and to pray for world peace. As part of the ritual, they add the names of those who were newly registered in the lists.

The two cities have different requirements for who can be included in their lists. In both, relatives of the deceased apply to register the deaths. Hiroshima accepts applications for all victims, regardless of nationality or whether they were recognised by the government as being hibakusha, or atomic bomb survivors. Nagasaki accepts only those who had been recognised by the government as being hibakusha. (Read more about this here)

There is no clear record of exactly how many people were killed. By the end of the year, the bombs had killed about 140,000 in Hiroshima and 74,000 in Nagasaki, according to the Atomic Heritage Foundation. This is around 72 times the number of people killed in the September 11, 2001 attack, and is roughly equal to the population of Des Moines, Iowa.

In 2018, the latest year for which government data is available, 493,344 deaths were recorded in Hiroshima and Nagasaki's lists.



The names on the lists are not public. The stories of many victims of atomic bombs are lost - there are tombs in Hiroshima and Nagashima that house the ashes of tens of thousands of people whose identities are unknown, or whose entire families have died.

Some organisations collect testimonials and artwork by survivors. In many, people recount a blinding flash of light; the mushroom cloud towering over the city; and the "black rain" - the radioactive rain that fell after the bombing of Hiroshima. A 97-year-old woman, who was 24 at the time, recounts rubbing cucumber juice on her burns to ease the pain because they had no medication. Another survivor, who was 16 at the time, remembers seeing a dead boy standing an umbrella rack, as if leaning on it for support, and burned bodies that looked like mannequins.

Using information from the Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum and the National Peace Memorial Hall for the Atomic Bomb Victims in Hiroshima and Nagasaki, I compiled a database of 1,312 testimonials by atomic bomb survivors.

Most of the testimonials come from survivors who were between 0 and 30 years old at the time of the bombings. Here is a selection of their stories.



MASAKO NAKAMURA
Age at bombing: 5 months
Masako's mother went to Hiroshima city to look for her friends after the bombing, carrying Masako on her back. "My mother told me she breastfed a baby who had been exposed to radiation. But the baby died," says Masako. "I heard that so many people died, they had to dig holes and bury them."

SUSUMU KAWAHARA
Age at bombing: 0
Susumu's mother entered Nagasaki city two days after the bombing, carrying him on her back. She did not talk about the bombing for decades, until Susumu was in his sixties and diagnosed with cancer. "My wife and I, we don't have children. My mother felt so much guilt - that maybe we couldn't have children because I had been exposed to radiation."





























HIRONAKA MASAKI
Age at bombing: 5
Hironaka was playing in the river when the bomb hit Hiroshima. His father's back was burned and blistered, with countless pieces of glass sticking out from it. "Take out the glass," he said to Hironaka, but he was too young to do it well. The next day, his father died.





























TAKAKO OOBA
Age at bombing: 15
"There was a blue flash. My body floated, and I blacked out. When I regained consciousness, I was buried under rubble. 'Help,' I shouted, and someone rescued me. When I went outside, there was nothing - just fires. The next day, I found my mother. She told me my father had died."

AKIO KAWANO
Age at bombing: 15
"I saw a woman buried under rubble, being burned alive. I regret to this day that I couldn't help her."





























TOMIOKA ITSUE
Age at bombing: 25
"What I remember most clearly is the image of children crouching on the ground, afraid of the red flames burning the city."

TETSUO FUJITA
Age at bombing: 25
"I entered Hiroshima on (August) 15, picking up bones wearing just my loincloth. This exposed me to radiation, and before I knew it, I was bleeding from the gum. A pregnant woman who seemed fine right after the bombing was dead a week later."
