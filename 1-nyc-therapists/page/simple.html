Who's your shrink?

Thousands of therapists practice in New York City. Who are they? How much do they cost?

By Minami Funakoshi

Published July 10, 2019

Break-ups, new jobs, deaths in the family, a general sense of inadequacy - anything can send you spirilling down, and the solution seems obvious: Go see a shrink.

One in five adults in the United States, or about 47 million people, experience some sort of mental health disorder in a given year, according to the National Alliance on Mental Illness.

More than 6,000 therapists practice in New York City - who are they? What issues do they treat?

Mid-career therapists

As of July 9, 2019, more than 6,100 therapists listed on Psychology Today practice in New York City. Many of them are in their early-to-mid careers - therapists that graduated between 2007 and 2017 account for a fifth of all therapists in the city.

How much does therapy in NYC cost?

Need therapy? Be ready to shell out hundreds of dollars. Around 80 percent of therapists in New York City charge 100 dollars or more per session, with the average cost at a whopping 200 dollars. (This calculation excludes therapists who did not list prices on their Psychology Today bios).

Source: Psychology Today (data retrieved July 9, 2019)

Note: For therapists who list a range for what they charge per session, the average was calculated and shown in this chart. Therapists who do not list their cost per session have been excluded from this chart

Relationship issues

Relationships, family, and divorce are among the top issues in which therapists in NYC say they specialise - much more than anxiety or drugs.

CBT is in, psychoanalysis is out

CBT, which focuses on changing patterns in behaviour or thought, has been increasingly popular among newer therapists. One in four therapists who graduated between 2012-2017 practice CBT, whereas only five percent of therapists who graduated between 1957-1962 use this treatment. (Read more about CBT here)

Psychoanalysis, first developed by Sigmund Freud, traditionally focuses on investigating the unconscious mind and releasing repressed emotions, desires, and memories. It has become a controversial discipline, with many of its theories contested or debunked.

Psychoanalysis was hot in the 1950s and 1960s. One in five therapists who graduated between 1957-1962 practice psychoanalysis, compared to nine percent of therapists who graduated between 2012-2017. (Read more about psychoanalysis here)
